Lab / Data Graphics with Spreadsheets

Objectives

  1. To gain experience of the different types of charts and graphs commonly used to display data; 
  2. To critically assess charts and graphs in terms of the four principles of data graphics; 
  3. To practice iterative redesign of data graphics. 

In a nutshell...

Your task is to use public data on urban development in Melbourne to explore the range of charts and graphs you can construct with Google Sheets. You should begin to form the habit of assessing critically each chart you see in terms of the four principles of data graphics: data density, data correspondence, data integrity, and aesthetics.

The fine print...

You can complete this exercise individually, but ideally you should work with a classmate, and discuss together your work. You can start in the supervised lab session and continue as required in your own time. The exercise is split into 5 stages: getting the data, your first data graphic, critically assessing your data graphic, a more complex data graphic, and putting it all together. 

Stage 1: Get the data into Google Sheets

First, you will need to load the tabular data on urban development in Melbourne, used as a running example throughout this course, into Google Sheets. There are a number of different ways to do this. One method is summarized below. If you are unfamiliar with Google Sheets and feel you need more assistance in getting started or just a refresher, you can work through the following Lynda training videos. Feel free to skip these if you are already comfortable with spreadsheets like Excel and Google Sheets.

  • Creating, naming, and saving a spreadsheet, https://www.lynda.com/Sheets-tutorials/Creating-naming-saving-spreadsheet/163414/187750-4.html?org=rmit.edu.au
  • Importing and converting .csv ... data and files, https://www.lynda.com/Sheets-tutorials/Importing-converting-csv-tsv-tab-txt-data-files/163414/187756-4.html?org=rmit.edu.au

Once you are ready to begin working with your data:

  1. Download the data in .csv (comma separated variable) format direct from the Development Activity Melbourne website http://developmentactivity.melbourne.vic.gov.au.
  2. Open a new Google Sheet in Google Drive http://drive.google.com ("New..." -> "Google Spreadsheet").
  3. Import your downloaded .csv file into your Google spreadsheet ("File" -> "Import" -> "Upload").

Once you have your data uploaded, make sure you give your spreadsheet and the tab containing the data sensible names. Then have a look through your data, familiarize yourself with what is there. If you are in any doubt about what the different columns and data items mean, have a look through the metadata (literally "data about data") also accessible on the download site http://developmentactivity.melbourne.vic.gov.au. What do you notice about the data?

Stage 2: Your first data graphic

Sometimes for small and clean data sets, viewing raw data in tables can be a quick way of understanding the data. However, even for moderately large and "messy" data sets (such as your data) you need a data graphic to help someone understand the patterns in the data.

We'll start by building a chart of the number of completed buildings by year. We'll repeat the procedure to do this, which involves first some reformatting and summarizing of the data, multiple times. So please ask for help in the lab if you get stuck or confused doing this. You may also find the following Lynda videos helpful if you need additional background assistance:

  • Using pivot tables, https://www.lynda.com/Sheets-tutorials/Using-pivot-tables/163414/187786-4.html?org=rmit.edu.au
  • Creating charts in Google Sheets, https://www.lynda.com/Sheets-tutorials/Using-pivot-tables/163414/187786-4.html?org=rmit.edu.au

Once you are ready to dive in, here's the procedure for summarizing your data with a pivot table:

  1. Select all your raw data (Stage 1) and insert a new pivot table ("Insert" -> "Pivot table..."). A pivot table allows you to quickly summarize large data sets.
  2. To summarize the years in which buildings were completed, add the "year_completed" field to the "Rows" of your pivot table. Sort ascending, and uncheck the "Show totals" box (it makes no sense to add together the years!)
  3. Next to summarize the status of the buildings, add the "status" field to the "Columns of your pivot table. Again, uncheck the "Show totals" box, it is not meaningful in this context.
  4. Now we need to add some values into the table. The values we need are the counts of completed buildings. To do this, add the "status" field again, but this time to the "Values", and summarized by "COUNTA" (counts of buildings).
  5. Finally, the "year_completed" field is only relevant to buildings with "status" as "COMPLETED". So add a "Filter" with the "status" field, and only "COMPLETED" shown.

Almost there! Now you are ready to chart your data.

  1. Select the two columns, which should now contain your counts of completed buildings from 2002-2017. The "Insert" -> "Chart..."
  2. You will now be presented with the Chart editor, which offers you a wide range of different options for how to present your data. Insert as a column chart with the default settings.

Stage 3: Critically assess your data graphic

Often (but not always) the defaults in Google Charts are quite sensible. (Note that this is usually not the case for most spreadsheet and mapping programs. As we will see in later weeks, the defaults are frequently quite stupid). But there are always choices, even with sensible defaults. Look critically at your data graphic and find:

  • one problem with data density (hint: think "data/ink ratio")
  • one problem with data integrity (hint: look carefully at your pivot table and then again at your chart)
  • one problem with data correspondence (hint: how easy is it to compare the relative completed buildings in 2015 versus 2006?)
  • one problem with aesthetics (hint: in fact, the defaults for this simple chart pretty good, but think alignment, spacing, balance, could they be improved?)

Now, explore different options for your chart using Google Sheets. Using the "Chart editor" (if it has disappeared either create a new chart or click on your existing chart and select "Edit..."):

  • find one way to make your data density worse and another way to make it better.
  • find one way to make your data integrity worse and another way to make it better.
  • find one way to make your data correspondence worse and another way to make it better.
  • find one way to make your aesthetics worse and another way to make them better.

Next explore the different types of chart, and compare these with your (best) column chart in terms of the four principles of data graphics. You should construct and compare:

  • a bar chart,
  • a line chart,
  • an area chart,
  • a scatterplot,
  • a pie chart, and
  • a gauge chart (!)

Note that in all cases the point of the exercise is not simply to construct the data graphic, but to critically assess its suitability (in terms of the four principles of data graphics) and begin to iteratively redesign your data graphic to improve it.

For a bonus mark, you can export your best charts to an image, and upload them to the Lab 1 discussion board, along with a brief summary of why it is your best image, to share and comment with others in the class.

Stage 4: More complex data graphics

Use the techniques you have learned to construct some more sophisticated data graphics showing:

  • The total completed buildings by year by suburb, for the 5 suburbs Carlton, Docklands, East Melbourne, North Melbourne, and Melbourne (CBD);
  • The number of bike spaces associated with developments applied, approved, completed, and under construction across different suburbs.
  • The total proportion of residential dwellings associated with developments applied for from each suburb.
  • The number of floors of all developments in Melbourne by year. You might also try to incorporate the different suburbs in this data graphics. (Hint: this does not require a pivot table, you should use the raw data, and Google Sheets' defaults are not a good choice). 

Again, in each cases critically assess your data graphic with reference to the four principles of data graphics, and iteratively redesign your data graphic to improve it.

For a bonus mark, you can export your best charts to an image, and upload them to the Lab 1 discussion board, along with a brief summary of why it is your best image, to share and comment with others in the class.
